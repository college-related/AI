TMS

TMS (Truth Maintainence System) is a system that is used in a problem solving inference where the truth of the knowledge base must be tautology or else the system may give error.

For example: let's say we have a problem solving inference with the following data in the knowledge Base(KB)
P → Q
P → R
Q ⇔ T

Say we add a new knowledge ¬Q to the system, then the line Q ⇔ T becomes false or errorfull.


In such cases the TMS comes into play and manages the truth of the KB.



There are methods of doing so:

  • TMS
    • It is a simple TMS where if a contradicting knowledge is found then we retract all the errorfull lines of the KB and refill with updated values.
    • This method is not much practical as it may take enormous amount of time.
    • In the above example if we had assertedTo put in KB ¬P then all the lines would be errorfull and assume if we had 100's of lines of fact with P then we have to retract all those lines and re enter the correct one, which is time consuming and not practical.
  • JTMS (Justification Based TMS)
    • JTMS is a modification to simple TMS where we have a justification for each fact we assert into the KB
    • In above example we can do:
      P
      {P, P → Q}
      {P, P → R}
      {P, Q ⇔ T}
      Then all the lines of fact with P as the justification can be marked as OUT which means the fact is not used anymore in the system.
    • The advantage of using justification is: we can easily find which facts are errorfull than in simple TMS, we can mark the contradicting facts as OUT and mark them IN which means the fact are used again when the fact are again correct.
  • ATMS (Assumption Based TMS)
    • ATMS is a modified version of JTMS where we can keep multiple set of possibly contradicting assumptions in the memory
    • It can simultaneously deal with different set of beliefs that may be mutually contradictory and investigate their consequences



Reasoning under uncertainty

The problem that an Reasoning system faces doesnot always have a certainty to their solution. For example: random problems like Filiping coin, or other real life problems like weather forecasting all have that uncertainty in them.

Those type of problem doesnot have either enough domain knowledge or have some randomness in them that makes the solution uncertain.
Though an Reasoning system must be complete which means it must provide a solution if there exists one, the fliping of coin have a solution either head or tail and the weather forecast also have a solution either rain or not, sunny or not. Hence Reasoning under uncertainty is important and must be in any Reasoning agent system.

The two methods of Reasoning under uncertainty are:

  • Non-monotonic Reasoning: Extending the facts or rules to reason with incomplete information, such as default reasoning.
  • Statistical Reasoning: Representation is extended to allow some kind of numeric measure of certainty(rather than simply ture or false), example: 70% chance of raining rather than saying it will rain.
